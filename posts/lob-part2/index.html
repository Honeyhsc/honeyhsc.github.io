EDA Limit Order Book - Part 2

Recap from Part 1:

In the previous blog(first part of this series), we understood the scale and volume of both Trade and Order Book data and understood Trade data: ~25,000 records per day (~8 trades every 10 seconds), while LoB data: ~210,000 records per day (~750 snapshots every 10 seconds). We looked at OHLC and found that the stock reached peak price around mid feb and followed a downward trend thereafter. Complementing this, we also saw spread consistently widened and traded volumes showed upwnward trend, we concluded that it gave strong indication of liquidity issues. We then discussed the thought process and the next logical step will be to analyze the trades data in further detail.

Trade data analysis:

Lets start by looking at the inter-trade durations, analyse its distribution and then dig-in further to see if there are any patterns especially bursts of activity.

The following plot shows the distribution of inter-trade durations, which is the time between consecutive trades. The x-axis represents the duration in seconds, while the y-axis shows the frequency of trades occurring within that duration.

As the durations seems to be skewed towards the left, we can see that most trades occur within a few seconds of each other, with a significant number of trades happening within the first 10 seconds. This does seem to indicate there could be bursts of trading activity, where multiple trades occur in quick succession. The plot also shows a long tail, indicating that there are some trades that occur after longer durations, but these are less frequent. The plot shows a striking similarity to an exponential distribution, which is often observed in financial markets. It is worth to quickly compare this with a Q-Q plot to confirm the distribution.

Quantiles of inter-event durations compared with quantiles of an exponential distribution with the same mean. The dashed line represents the perfect fit line exponential distribution fitted to the observed durations. Observed durations are found to be quite similar till the 11 sec mark, after which points representing the trade events that take too long >10s are talking longer than what it take in a true exponantial distribution. Looking at the first graph, such trades are rare. Knowing that the exponential distribution is a good fit for the inter-event durations, we can use it to model the time between trades. This can be useful for predicting future trade events and also simulating trade events in a market model. Knowing that trades are following Poisson process for the most part, indicates that in this case trades show a memoryless property, meaning that the time until the next trade does not depend on how long it has been since the last trade. This open up possibiliites like time-weighted average price (TWAP) strategies, where traders can place orders at regular intervals to achieve a desired average price over a specified time period. Also if inter-event duration deviates significantly from the exponential distribution, it may indicate a useful signal for trading strategies, such as identifying periods of increased market activity or potential price movements. I also see possibilities of using time-based models like ACD (Autoregressive Conditional Duration) models as strong baseline to capture the dynamics of inter-event durations and improve trading strategies based on the observed patterns in the data. We will return to this in the next part of the series. For now lets go back to confirm the bursts of activity we saw in the first plot.
