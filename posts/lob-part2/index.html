Recap from Part 1:

In the previous blog, we explored the scale and structure of both Trade and Limit Order Book (LoB) data. We discovered:

  • Trade data: ~25,000 records per day, ~8 trades every 10 seconds
  • LoB data: ~210,000 records per day, ~750 snapshots every 10 seconds

We used OHLC charts to show that the stock reached a peak around mid-February and declined steadily thereafter. At the same time, we saw widening bid-ask spreads and a drop in traded volume, we concluded that it gave strong indication of deteriorating liquidity and growing market stress.

With this foundation we further discussed the thought process and the next logical step will be to analyze the trades data in further detail, particularly the timing between trades, to understand market rhythm and detect potential signals for price prediction.

Trade Data Analysis: Inter-Trade Durations

Let’s begin by analyzing the inter-trade durations — the time difference between consecutive trades. This tells us whether trades occur in a steady flow or arrive in bursts, and it’s critical for modeling market activity.

Distribution of Inter-Trade Durations

The plot below shows the distribution of inter-trade intervals (in seconds):

We observe a right-skewed distribution:

  • Most trades happen within a few seconds of each other.
  • There’s a long tail extending beyond 10 seconds, indicating occasional trading lulls.
  • The shape resembles an exponential distribution, which is often linked to Poisson processes in market modeling.

Q-Q Plot: Comparison with Exponential Distribution

To validate the exponential assumption, we compare the observed durations to a theoretical exponential distribution:

The Q-Q plot shows:

  • A close fit up to ~11 seconds
  • Beyond that, actual durations are longer than expected
  • This suggests non-random bursts of inactivity that deviate from pure memorylessness

Implications for Modeling and Trading

The fit to an exponential distribution suggests that, in general, trade arrivals can be modeled as a memoryless Poisson process. This has several key implications:

  • Time-weighted execution strategies (e.g., TWAP) may be suitable for this type of flow
  • Long deviations from the expected duration (e.g., >10s) may indicate a useful signal for trading strategies, such as identifying periods of increased market activity or potential price movements.
  • Autoregressive Conditional Duration (ACD) models can be used to better capture the dynamics of trade timing, especially under bursty or irregular conditions

We’ll explore these modeling opportunities in future posts. But for now, let’s follow up by visually confirming whether these bursts occur in time-localized patterns, and whether they have any relationship with price volatility or trade volume.
